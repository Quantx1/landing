## 4. Leverage and Derivatives (Futures and Options) Risk

4.1. **Equity derivatives are high-risk, leveraged instruments and are not suitable for all users.** Because derivatives are traded on margin, a relatively small adverse price movement in the underlying can cause a large loss relative to the margin you have deposited. **Leverage magnifies both gains and losses.**

4.2. **Options can, and frequently do, expire worthless.** If you buy an option, the entire premium you pay is at risk and you can lose 100% of it if the option expires out of the money.

4.3. **Selling (writing) options and holding futures positions can expose you to large — and in the case of certain option-selling strategies, theoretically unlimited — losses**, which can substantially exceed the premium received or the initial margin deposited. Adverse gap movements, volatility spikes, and expiry-day ("zero-day"/weekly-expiry) dynamics can cause rapid, outsized losses.

4.4. Your broker may levy **margin calls** and may **square off (liquidate) your positions**, in whole or in part, without prior notice, if your available margin falls short of exchange or broker requirements. Such forced liquidation may occur at unfavourable prices and may crystallise losses. Margin requirements can change intraday and at short notice.

4.5. Derivatives are further exposed to **time decay (theta), volatility risk (vega), sensitivity to underlying price (delta/gamma), and interest-rate effects**, as well as expiry, settlement, and assignment mechanics. Understanding these factors is your responsibility.

4.6. Any tool the Platform provides in relation to derivatives (for example, an options screener, strategy builder, payoff visualiser, or Greeks display) is **informational only** and does not measure, limit, or manage the risk in your account. Actual outcomes depend on live prices, liquidity, margins, and execution on your broker account.

## 5. Algorithmic and Automated-Trading Risks

5.1. **Model risk.** The Platform's signal engines, AI copilot, and analytics are built on statistical and machine-learning models trained on historical data. Models are simplifications of reality; they can be wrong, can degrade as market conditions change ("model drift"), and offer **no guarantee** that past statistical relationships will continue. Model outputs may be confidently stated yet incorrect.

5.2. **AI/LLM-specific risk.** The AI copilot and chat features may produce responses that are inaccurate, incomplete, outdated, biased, or fabricated ("hallucinated"). AI outputs must be independently verified before you rely on them. They are not advice and must not be treated as a substitute for your own judgement or that of a qualified, appropriately registered professional.

5.3. **Erroneous, duplicate, or unintended orders.** Automated or semi-automated order placement can generate erroneous, duplicate, mistimed, or unintended orders due to software defects, configuration errors, incorrect parameters, data errors, or user mistakes. Such orders may execute before they can be cancelled and may cause losses.

5.4. **Latency and timing risk.** There is unavoidable delay between signal generation, your decision, order transmission, and execution at the exchange. Prices can move materially during this interval, so the price at which an order actually fills may differ significantly from the price shown when a signal or suggestion was produced.

5.5. **Connectivity and infrastructure risk.** Automated workflows depend on internet connectivity, your device, our servers, third-party cloud infrastructure, broker APIs, and exchange systems, any of which may be slow, interrupted, or unavailable. A failure at any point may cause missed entries or exits, unhedged or unintended exposure, or an inability to modify or cancel orders.

5.6. **Over-optimisation and overfitting.** Strategies and parameters that appear strong on historical data may be **overfitted** — that is, tuned to noise in the past rather than to durable, repeatable behaviour. Overfitted strategies commonly perform far worse in live markets than in testing. Any statistical gate, Sharpe filter, or walk-forward check applied by the Platform reduces but does **not eliminate** this risk.

5.7. **Automation does not remove responsibility.** Even where the Platform can place orders on your connected broker account, **you remain fully responsible for every order and position in your account.** You should monitor automated activity, understand each strategy before enabling it, use appropriate stop-losses and position limits at the broker level, and be prepared to intervene manually. You should also be aware of, and comply with, all applicable rules governing algorithmic trading and API-based order placement that apply to you and your broker.

## 6. Backtesting, Paper Trading, and Paper-vs-Live Divergence

6.1. **Backtests are hypothetical.** Backtest and simulated results are computed on historical data with the benefit of hindsight and a fixed set of assumptions. They do **not** represent actual trading and are **no guarantee** of future or live results.

6.2. Backtests may not fully capture real-world frictions, including **brokerage, exchange transaction charges, STT/CTT, stamp duty, GST, SEBI turnover fees, slippage, bid-ask spreads, market impact, borrowing/margin costs, taxes, and the limits of available liquidity.** They may also be affected by survivorship bias, look-ahead bias, data errors, and corporate-action adjustments.

6.3. **Paper trading uses virtual funds** and is provided free for learning and experimentation. Paper-trading fills are simulated and typically assume favourable execution. **Paper-trading results routinely differ — often substantially — from live results** because live trading involves real order queues, partial fills, rejections, slippage, margin constraints, latency, emotions, and costs that a simulation cannot fully reproduce.

6.4. A strategy that performs well in backtesting or paper trading **may lose money in live trading.** Do not treat historical or simulated performance as an indication that you will achieve similar results with real capital.

## 8. Liquidity, Slippage, and Execution Risk

8.1. **Liquidity risk.** Some securities and derivatives contracts trade thinly. In illiquid instruments you may be unable to enter or exit a position at the size or price you want, or at all, particularly during volatile conditions or near expiry.

8.2. **Slippage.** The price at which an order actually executes may differ from the last-traded, quoted, or displayed price. Market orders in particular can fill at prices materially worse than expected, especially in fast-moving or thin markets.

8.3. **Gaps and volatility.** Prices can gap sharply between the previous close and the next open, or during the session, on news or events. **Stop-loss orders are not guaranteed** to execute at the stop price and may fill at a substantially worse level, or not at all, during gaps or halts.

8.4. **Partial fills and rejections.** Orders may be only partially filled or may be rejected by the broker or exchange (for example, due to margin shortfalls, price bands, freeze quantities, or surveillance limits), leaving you with unintended exposure or no position when you expected one.
